Overview

Traditional risk models can't price the unknown — this framework stress-tests architecture instead of estimating probabilities.

Risk assessment built on historical failure data and CVE databases fails against zero-day threats: vulnerabilities with no data, no signatures, no priors. Rather than asking "what is the probability of an intrusion?", this work asks "how does the system behave if an unknown vector compromises a critical component?"

The framework enriches a digital twin into a security twin (ST) — an attribute-rich graph of assets, network topology, ACLs, and vulnerability states that generates the full state space of possible intrusions. Hypothetical zero-day vulnerabilities (CVSS 10.0, deterministic exploit) are injected into the twin only — the real CPS is never touched — and extensive Monte Carlo adversary simulations measure the resulting degradation via Mean Time to Compromise (MTTC) and Intrusion Success Percentage (IS%).

Headline result: the topological centrality of the compromised asset predicts systemic failure far better than the vulnerability's intrinsic severity. The same CVSS-10 zero-day cuts MTTC by 88% on an internal pivot, versus 65% on an endpoint.

Reading the results

The baseline confirms that segmentation and patch management hold against known CVEs: attackers must chain low-probability exploits, giving defenders a ~3-day window. A zero-day on the internal pivot (Central File Server) collapses that window to 8.9 hours with minimal variance — a deterministic-like failure, because the CFS's betweenness centrality bridges the Enterprise zone straight into OT management. The endpoint scenario is path-dependent: its high MTTC variance means outcomes hinge on whether the attacker lands on a privileged user's workstation.

Key findings

Three conclusions for resilience engineering.

FINDING 01

Location beats severity

With severity held constant at CVSS 10.0, systemic risk varied by a factor of ~3 in MTTC purely by injection location. Betweenness centrality — not the score — is the risk predictor that matters.

FINDING 02

Pivot points are force multipliers

Assets bridging the IT/OT divide (file servers, backup paths) act as high-speed conduits into the control zone. Hardening and micro-segmenting these nodes yields a higher resilience ROI than indiscriminate patching.

FINDING 03

MTTC is a design metric

An 8.9-hour compromise window creates a race most human-driven response teams cannot win — a quantitative argument for automated isolation of high-centrality nodes on anomalous behavior.

Methodology

Discrete-step adversary simulation over a formally defined security twin.

1

Formalize the security twin

The ST is a directed, attribute-rich graph defining every action a threat actor can execute given its acquired information and access rights. Vertices are cyber-physical assets with zone, criticality, and vulnerability state; edges are logical reachability permitted by firewalls and routing; weights are exploitability probabilities.

G = (V, E, Λ),   si = ⟨Zone, Crit, Vuln⟩,   Λ : E → [0, 1]
2

Inject the zero-day perturbation

Under the "Assume Breach" paradigm, a hypothetical zero-day z₀ is added to a target asset's vulnerability set as a deterministic transition — a "gravity well" in the state space. Only the twin is modified; the operational CPS is untouched.

P(exploit | z₀) ≈ 1.0,   Severity(z₀) = 10.0
3

Simulate a rational adversary

A utility-maximizing agent picks its next target among reachable neighbors, weighing exploit reliability against asset value. Action durations are Log-Normal with mean inversely tied to exploitability — low-probability exploits cost trial-and-error time, zero-days are near-instant.

vnext = argmaxv∈N(Ct) P(Ct→v) · Value(v),   Tcomp ~ LogNorm(μ, σ²),  μ ∝ 1/Λ(e)
4

Run Monte Carlo to convergence

Simulations repeat until the 95% confidence interval of the metrics falls within a 5% relative error margin, ensuring the intrusion state space is adequately explored before reporting IS% and MTTC.

1.96 · σ / √N ≤ 0.05 · μ

Limitations

The adversary is a rational speed-optimizer — deceptive "low-and-slow" state-sponsored strategies are out of scope — and defenses are static during an intrusion (no mid-attack patching or isolation). Future work integrates multi-agent reinforcement learning to model the attacker–defender interaction dynamically.
